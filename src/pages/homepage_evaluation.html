🔍 Lens 1 — Senior Recruiter

90-second test

Would I keep reading? → Yes — but with hesitation.

The visual design passes immediately. Dark mode, vibrant project cards,professional mockups. It signals craft. What creates hesitation is the hero copy. "Great questions drive transformation" sounds like a philosophy poster, not a value proposition. A recruiter skimming 50 portfolios a day reads this and thinks "okay, but what do you actually do?"

What gets this portfolio interviews ✓

  1. Visual design quality — The colour palette, typography hierarchy, and mockup quality immediately signal craft seniority. Recruiters forward this to hiring managers with "looks great."
  2. Company brand recognition — Springer Nature + Farfetch are name-brand environments that shortcut credibility. These are doing a lot of work.
  3. Project diversity — eCommerce, dating, discovery, B2B research platform. Shows range without feeling scattered.

What gets this portfolio skipped ✗

  1. Zero numbers on the homepage — There are no metrics anywhere visible. Recruiters expect Senior designers to know their impact. Without a single number, this looks like a portfolio of outputs, not outcomes.
  2. Hero headline doesn't position you — "Great questions drive transformation" could be on a motivational calendar. It says nothing about what Renan makes, for whom, or what kind of problems he's best at.
  3. Locked/missing projects — Two of four projects are unavailable. Half the portfolio is blocked. This is a major conversion problem.

Highest-leverage next step

Add one metric to each project thumbnail title. Even a single number transforms a descriptive title into a proof point. "Redesigning the mobile experience of a dating platform" → "Redesigning a dating app's mobile experience — +38% match engagement."

The craft would pass a Staff-level visual bar at most companies. But craft without impact reads as a strong mid-level designer, not a Senior one.

Critical gap

The homepage shows outputs (what was built) but not outcomes (what changed). A senior-caliber homepage should make a hiring manager feel: "this person understands the business impact of design." Right now it makes them feel: "this person makes beautiful things." That's necessary but not sufficient at Senior level.

Highest-leverage fix

Add a one-line impact statement below each project title on the homepage. It doesn't need to be long. "↑ conversion rate for 50+ luxury brands" is enough.

✍️ Lens 3 — Senior Copywriter

The 3-second rule applied

Headline: "Great questions drive transformation" — Weak

  • ✅ It has rhythm. It's readable. It's not ugly.
  • ❌ It's not specific. "Transformation" is one of the most overused words in design and business.
  • ❌ It doesn't anchor to design.
  • ❌ It leads with a method ("questions") not a outcome or identity.

Headline rewrites

Original: "Great questions drive transformation"

Option A — Leads withoutcome/impact:
"The designer who turns hard questions into measurable product outcomes."
Option B — Leads with identity/positioning:
"Designing at the intersection of scale and craft — for products that earn loyalty."

Subheadline rewrite

Original: "Hiya, I'm Renan. 👋 A Senior Product Designer who turns relentless curiosity into State-of-the-art products. Currently @Springer Nature. Previously @Farfetch."

Rewrite:
"I design products for the complex, the ambiguous, and the high-stakes — most recently at Springer Nature, previously scaling Farfetch's luxury eCommerce experience."

Project title rewrites

Project 1: Farfetch

  • Problem-led: "The craftsmanship gap killing luxury brand conversions at scale"
  • Outcome-led: "How I lifted performance for Farfetch's top fashion brands across 50+ storefronts"

Project 2: Dating app

  • Problem-led: "When a dating app's mobile experience made connection feel impossible"
  • Outcome-led: "Redesigning a dating platform's mobile UX — from friction to engagement"

📊 Lens 4 — Senior Product Manager Evaluator

What a PM sees when looking at this homepage

A Product Manager evaluating a design collaborator notices:

Green flags ✓

  • "Driving platform growth through targeted researcher acquisition" is the most PM-aligned title. "Growth" and "acquisition" are product/business language.
  • Two marquee companies (Farfetch, Springer Nature) in genuinely product-complex environments.

Red flags ✗

  • No metrics, anywhere. From a PM's perspective, this is the most critical gap. PMs think in terms of impact.
  • Zero mention of cross-functional work. PMs want to know: did this designer partner with engineers, data, research?
  • "Improving the performance" — what does that mean? Conversion rate? Page load? Revenue? Vague language for a PM.

Highest-leverage fix from a PM's lens

Add a two-line "impact statement" to each project card that frames outcomes in business language. Not "I redesigned X" — but "X led to Y for Z users/customers/the business."
